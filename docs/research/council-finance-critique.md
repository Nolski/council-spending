# Where the money goes: a critical analysis of Devon & Exeter council finance

*A political-economy reading of public spending data for Devon County Council and
Exeter City Council, written to support an evidence-based re-budgeting argument.*

## How to read this report (methodology & confidence)

This report mixes three kinds of evidence, labelled throughout:

- **[Verified]** — claims independently fact-checked against authoritative primary
  sources (IFS, NAO, Institute for Government, House of Commons Library, council
  Statements of Accounts, DfE, CHPI, CLES). High confidence.
- **[Our data]** — figures computed from the councils' own published *spend-over-£500*
  (Devon) and *spend-over-£250* (Exeter) transparency files in this project. These are
  **directional**: our category classifier covers ~59% of spend by value; sole-trader
  payee names are redacted at source; the SEND home-to-school transport category is
  under-captured and the "care provider" category may over-capture. Treat magnitudes as
  indicative, not audited.
- **[Reported]** — figures from credible secondary reporting we have cited but not fully
  cross-checked; flagged where confidence is lower.

A core structural fact governs everything below: **this is a two-tier area.** Devon
County Council runs adult social care, children's services/SEND, education, public
health, highways and waste *disposal*. Exeter City Council runs housing, homelessness
and temporary accommodation, leisure, planning and waste *collection*. NHS budgets are
neither council's spend. Each issue is therefore evidenced only from the council that
actually holds the function.

## 1. The funding context: managed decline, by design

The financial pressure on both councils is not primarily a story of local
mismanagement. It is the local expression of a deliberate national choice.

- **[Verified]** English councils' core funding (central grant + local taxes) in
  2024-25 is **~9% lower in real terms than 2010-11 — an 18% cut per resident** once
  population growth is counted.
  [IFS](https://ifs.org.uk/publications/how-have-english-councils-funding-and-spending-changed-2010-2024),
  [IFS news](https://ifs.org.uk/news/core-funding-english-councils-still-18-lower-resident-2010-11-and-costs-are-rising).
- **[Verified]** The cuts were **regressive by design**: funding per person fell ~26% in
  the most deprived tenth of areas versus ~11% in the least deprived (the 2010s gap was
  32.8% vs 13.7%), reversing decades of redistribution.
  [IFS](https://ifs.org.uk/publications/how-have-english-councils-funding-and-spending-changed-2010-2024),
  [Institute for Government](https://www.instituteforgovernment.org.uk/publication/performance-tracker-2025/local-services/overview).
- **[Verified]** Funding was **regressively re-localised**: council tax rose from ~36% of
  core funding (2010-11) to ~53% (2024-25), and devolved business rates do not track
  social-care need, so a council's resources are now decoupled from its needs. Council
  tax is capped at ~2–5% a year without a referendum, so the lever is weak and
  regressive.
  [IFS — present model unsustainable](https://ifs.org.uk/articles/present-model-funding-local-government-unsustainable),
  [Commons Library](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/why-are-local-authorities-going-bankrupt/).
- **[Verified]** **Social care has crowded out everything else**: adult + children's
  social care rose to ~65% of (non-education) budgets in 2023-24 (from ~50% in 2010-11),
  while housing spending fell by a third and highways, culture/leisure and planning each
  fell 40–50%; youth services and Sure Start fell ~70%.
  [IFS](https://ifs.org.uk/publications/how-have-english-councils-funding-and-spending-changed-2010-2024).
- **[Verified]** **Section 114 notices are a statutory duty, not bankruptcy** — the
  finance officer *must* issue one when a balanced budget cannot be set. After lying
  dormant since 2000, they clustered from 2018 (8 councils, 12 notices, 2018–2023), and
  central "Exceptional Financial Support" ballooned from £79.9m (2018/19) to £1.6bn
  (2024/25). CIPFA's chief executive warns even a well-run council can "hit the buffers
  through a lack of funding."
  [Commons Library CBP-8520](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-8520/),
  [Institute for Government](https://www.instituteforgovernment.org.uk/publication/performance-tracker-2025/local-services/overview).
- **[Verified]** **Reserves are being burned to stay afloat**: unallocated reserves fell
  £4.9bn→£3.8bn and unringfenced £35.8bn→£24.6bn (2020/21→2023/24); three-quarters of
  councils used reserves to balance 2023-24 budgets. An unsustainable signal of distress.
  [NAO Local Government Financial Sustainability (Feb 2025)](https://www.nao.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/local-government-financial-sustainability-summary.pdf).

**What our data shows.** [Our data] Devon's published spending totals ~£9.65bn across
2011–2026; Exeter's ~£318m across 2021–2026. The composition (below) reflects exactly the
national pattern — demand-led statutory services (care, SEND) dominating while
discretionary services are squeezed.

**Re-budgeting options.** At this level the binding constraints are national: a fairer
funding settlement, restored needs-based grant, and multi-year certainty. Locally, the
honest framing is *damage limitation plus stopping avoidable leakage* — which is what the
rest of this report quantifies.

## 2. Housing & homelessness — Exeter (district)

**The critical reading.** Right to Buy depleted the social housing stock while it was
never replaced one-for-one; a frozen Local Housing Allowance and reliance on the private
rented sector then converted a one-off *capital* problem (build/own homes) into a
permanent *revenue* transfer to private landlords and B&B operators. Nationally, councils
spent ~£2.29bn on temporary accommodation in 2023/24, with nightly-paid/B&B spend rising
roughly five-fold since 2017-18.
[Crisis](https://www.crisis.org.uk/about-us/crisis-media-centre/council-spending-on-emergency-accommodation-tops-22bn/),
[Commons Library CBP-10421](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-10421/).

- **[Verified]** Exeter maintains **89 contracted units** (private landlords + housing
  associations) and **41 council-owned units**, and spot-purchases hotel/B&B rooms when
  these are full.
  [Exeter CC](https://exeter.gov.uk/homelessness-awareness-week/homelessness-and-temporary-accommodation/).
- **[Reported / unverified]** A figure of 600+ households in temporary accommodation
  circulated in earlier scoping; the council's public pages do not state a current count
  or annual cost. **Open item:** pull the number and cost from an Exeter Housing
  committee budget paper before publishing.

**What our data shows.** [Our data] Classified temporary-accommodation spend in Exeter's
files is ~£6.0m (2021–2026) — but this is an **undercount**: payee redaction hides
sole-trader landlords, and Exeter only publishes payments over £250. It establishes the
*direction* (recurring revenue flowing to private/hotel providers), not the full total.

**Council housing & rent revenue (the HRA).** This is the user's specific interest and a
genuine data gap. Council-housing **rent income** sits in the ring-fenced Housing Revenue
Account — it is *income*, not the *expenditure* our transaction files capture — and stock
numbers / Right-to-Buy sales come from separate statutory returns. **Open item (Phase 4):
** ingest Exeter's HRA Statement of Accounts (dwelling stock, rent income, RTB receipts)
and gov.uk social-housing-stock / RTB-sales statistics to evidence "availability + rent
revenue" directly. Our spend data cannot show this on its own.

**Re-budgeting options.** Compare the *recurring* cost of leasing/spot-purchasing TA
against the *amortised* cost of acquiring or building council homes (a "rent-vs-build"
ratio). Where the lifetime cost of building is lower — which rising TA spend increasingly
implies — capital investment in council stock is the fiscally conservative choice, not the
radical one.

## 3. Adult social care — Devon (county); the council side of NHS pressure

**The critical reading.** A decade of cuts left an adult-social-care funding gap.
Insufficient home- and residential-care capacity drives **delayed hospital discharges**,
so a social-care shortfall presents downstream as an A&E / bed-blocking problem. The NHS
budget itself is not council spend — but the council-funded care that would relieve it is.
[LGA / NHS Confederation](https://www.local.gov.uk/publications/exploring-adult-social-care-funding-and-delayed-discharge).

**What our data shows.** [Our data] Care/social-care provider payments are **by far
Devon's largest classified category — ~£3.48bn cumulative** across the period, consistent
with adult social care being the council's biggest budget line. (Caveat: our broad "care"
matching may over-capture; treat as the upper end of a range.) The structural point holds
regardless of the exact figure: the single biggest call on county money is care, and most
of that capacity is privately provided (see §5).

**Re-budgeting options.** The lever here is **how** care is bought, not whether: shifting
spend toward in-house, not-for-profit and locally-owned providers; commissioning for
quality and stability rather than lowest unit price; and investing in reablement /
"discharge-to-assess" capacity that reduces the most expensive placements. Every pound
retained from profit extraction (§5) is a pound kept in front-line care.

## 4. Education & SEND — Devon (county)

**The critical reading.** Academisation removed schools from council control while
councils kept their SEND duties, and an underfunded high-needs block produced a
structural deficit nationally (~9 in 10 councils in deficit). Spend on **private special
schools** and **home-to-school transport** has risen steeply because inclusive state
provision was hollowed out — public money flowing to private operators by default.
[IFS — Spending on SEN in England](https://ifs.org.uk/publications/spending-special-educational-needs-england-something-has-change).

- **[Verified]** Devon signed a DfE **"Safety Valve" agreement on 21 March 2024**: DfE
  provides **~£95m extra high-needs funding over eight years**; the council commits ~£5m/yr
  from revenue plus ~£20m from reserves; the forecast DSG deficit (~£153–163m, peaking
  ~£160.9m in 2025-26) is to be *reduced* toward ~£95m by 2031-32.
  [Devon CC](https://www.devon.gov.uk/news/government-cash-support-for-devons-special-needs-education/).
  **Correction to earlier scoping:** there is **no ~£180m "write-off"** — the deficit is
  partly *covered*, not erased — and the "£50m cuts / £13m asset sales" detail is
  secondary-sourced (TES), not in the council's own statement. The deal is already under
  strain (2024-25 high-needs overspend ~£45.8m, ~£14.7m over target).

**What our data shows.** [Our data] Classified private special-school placement spend is
~£353m cumulative in Devon's files. SEND home-to-school **transport is under-captured** by
our current classifier (a known limitation — the rule needs refining against Devon's
actual expense descriptions), so that figure is not yet reliable.

**Re-budgeting options.** The Safety Valve framing treats the deficit as a *spending*
problem to be cut down to size. The alternative framing: sustained capital investment in
inclusive state special provision reduces the long-run unit cost of private placements and
transport — a cheaper steady state than perpetual outsourced placements, if central
funding rules allow it.

## 5. Public money into private profit — both councils

This is the area where the data speaks most clearly, and where local decisions have most
room to act. Outsourcing, long-term private-finance contracts, agency staffing and
consultancy all route public money to private firms — sometimes for genuine capacity,
sometimes as straightforward leakage.

**Private finance (PFI).**
- **[Verified] Devon County Council *does* hold PFI** (correcting earlier scoping that
  assumed none): an **Exeter Schools PFI** (28-year, 5 secondary + 1 primary; closing
  liability ~£39.6m in 2022-23) and **Energy-from-Waste PFI** contracts under the Devon
  Waste Partnership (further liabilities in the tens of millions). Unitary charges split
  into service, interest and liability-repayment elements over the life of the deals.
  [Devon Statement of Accounts 2022-23, Note 35](https://democracy.devon.gov.uk/documents/s48154/Statement%20of%20Accounts%202022-23.pdf).
- **[Verified — none found] Exeter City Council:** no PFI contract identified; the
  schools/waste PFIs sit with the county, not the city.
  [Exeter contracts](https://exeter.gov.uk/council-and-democracy/council-information/council-data/contracts/).

**The financialised care market (national context for Devon's £3.48bn).**
- **[Verified]** The Centre for Health and the Public Interest's *Plugging the Leaks*
  study of 830+ care companies found **~£1.5bn a year (~10% of sector income) leaks out**
  as profit, debt repayments, rent and management fees. Among the 26 largest providers,
  ~£117m (45% of debt-related payments) flows to **related, often offshore, companies**;
  the five largest private-equity-owned providers carry ~£35,000 debt per bed, with
  interest of ~£102/bed/week (~16% of fees). Most beds are for-profit; the Four Seasons
  collapse (45,000 residents) is the cautionary case.
  [CHPI, *Plugging the Leaks*](https://www.chpi.org.uk/reports/plugging-the-leaks-in-the-uk-care-home-industry).
  This is the mechanism by which a large share of Devon's care spend can leave the local
  economy as financial return rather than front-line care.

**What our data shows.** [Our data] Across both councils, classified outsourced-spend
categories include **agency staff ~£286m, management consultants ~£178m, IT systems
~£118m, and highways/construction ~£1.03bn** (cumulative, both councils). On
**concentration**, Exeter's **top-10 suppliers take ~53% of its annual spend** versus
~18–26% for Devon (across ~6,000 suppliers) — a much narrower base of beneficiaries at
the district level. Exeter's largest single contract is **Mears' integrated asset
management (~£64m)**. Figures are directional (59% classification coverage; redaction).

**Re-budgeting options.** This is the most actionable section. Levers: **insource** where
in-house delivery is cheaper and more stable; reduce agency/consultant reliance by
rebuilding permanent capacity; commission to retain value locally; and, for PFI, scrutinise
unitary charges and contract-end "handback" terms. Each pound of avoidable leakage recovered
is available for the care, housing and SEND pressures above.

## 6. The alternative: Community Wealth Building

The constructive frame for "re-budgeting" is **Community Wealth Building** — using the
council and other "anchor institutions" to retain wealth in the local economy through
procurement, employment and ownership.

- **[Verified]** **Preston** redirected anchor-institution procurement spend: the share
  retained *within Preston* rose from **5% (2012/13) to 18.2% (2016/17)** — about
  **£112.3m, a +£74m increase** — and the share retained *within Lancashire* rose from 39%
  to 79.2% (+~£200m), against a ~£750m baseline across six institutions.
  [CLES — The Preston Model](https://cles.org.uk/the-preston-model/),
  [CLES report PDF](https://cles.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/2019/07/CLES_Preston-Document_WEB-AW.pdf).

**What a council can and can't do.** Procurement rules now permit weighting for social
value and local economic benefit, so progressive procurement, insourcing and supporting
cooperative/local suppliers are lawful levers. What a council *cannot* do alone is fix the
national funding settlement, the SEND funding formula, or LHA freezes — so the honest
argument pairs **local action on leakage and procurement** with **advocacy for structural
national reform**.

**Re-budgeting priorities implied by this analysis:**
1. **Stop the leak** — cut avoidable agency/consultant spend and scrutinise PFI/large
   contracts; redirect savings to statutory pressures.
2. **Build, don't rent** — invest capital in council housing and inclusive SEND provision
   to lower long-run revenue costs (TA, private placements, transport).
3. **Keep it local** — progressive procurement and community wealth building to retain
   spend in the Devon/Exeter economy.
4. **Commission care for stability, not lowest price** — reduce profit leakage from the
   single largest budget line.

## Limitations

- **Our figures are directional.** They derive from published transparency data
  (spend over £500 / £250), cover ~59% of spend by value after classification, and exclude
  redacted sole-trader payees. The "care" category may over-capture; "SEND transport" is
  under-captured. They show direction and order-of-magnitude, not audited totals.
- **Two-tier discipline.** Issues are attributed only to the council that holds the
  function; cross-council supplier totals are noted as such.
- **Council housing / HRA, reserves trajectory, and the precise TA household count and
  cost are not yet in the dataset** — these are flagged open items for the supplementary
  context layer (Phase 4), to be sourced from Statements of Accounts and gov.uk statistics.
- **Verification status is labelled** per claim. National context (§1) and the specific
  items in §4–§6 carry primary-source citations; items marked *[Reported]* or *open item*
  are not yet fully verified.
