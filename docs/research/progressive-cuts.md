# Progressive cuts: who should pay first (cited)

Companion to `council-finance-critique.md`, `revenue-levers.md` and `redirect-scenarios.md`.
Owning the assets in the revenue-levers dossier takes up-front money, and the first round has
to come from cuts. This document sets out **which** cuts — the principle being that council
tax already falls hardest on those with least, so the fairest first cuts fall on **wealth and
assets**, while the means-tested safety net is explicitly protected. Confidence labels:
**[Verified]** primary/official source fetched; **[Reported]** credible secondary.

## The principle: council tax is regressive, so lean the other way
- **[Verified] Council tax is regressive relative to property value.** A ~£100,000 home pays
  roughly **five times** the effective rate of a ~£1m home, and bands are still pegged to
  **1991 valuations**. So flat or across-the-board cuts/charges compound an already-regressive
  base; progressive levers correct for it.
  [Resolution Foundation](https://www.resolutionfoundation.org/comment/council-tax-is-a-farce-its-time-for-a-real-property-tax/).
- **[Reported] By income**, even after support the poorest tenth pay ~7–8% of income in
  council tax vs ~2–3% for the richest 40%.
  [IFS — council tax support and incomes](https://ifs.org.uk/publications/how-does-council-tax-support-shape-household-incomes-and-work-incentives).

## The levers (named, weighted toward those able to pay)

| Lever | Council | Falls on | Value | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Second-homes 100% premium | Exeter | Second-home owners | +£1.0m/yr (Exeter keeps ~£83k) | Adopted, Apr 2025 |
| Long-term empty-homes premium (after 1yr) | Exeter | Owners of empty homes | +£0.2m/yr (keeps ~£16k) | Adopted, Apr 2024 |
| DIY/construction-waste charges (tips) | Devon | Home-improvers/renovators | ~£1.0m/yr | In place (constrained 2024) |
| Garden-waste subscription | Exeter | Garden-owning households | £49–£61/yr opt-in | In place |
| Northbrook golf-course subsidy | Exeter | Golfers | part of £0.6m leisure cut | Proposed 2025 |
| Emissions-banded resident parking permits | Devon | Larger/multiple-car owners | £35 → £55–65 by band | In place; expandable |
| Means-tested ASC charges + deferred payments | Devon | Care users with assets >£23,250 | cost-recovery vs wealth | In place |

- **[Verified] Second & empty-homes premiums.** Exeter adopted the 100% second-homes premium
  (from Apr 2025, est. **+£1,024,557/yr** across all precepting bodies; Exeter's ~8% share
  ≈ £82,784) and a 100% empty-homes premium after one year (from Apr 2024, est. **+£201,829/yr**;
  Exeter share ≈ £16,307). Powers from the Levelling-up and Regeneration Act 2023.
  [Exeter CC report](https://committees.exeter.gov.uk/documents/s93519/Report%20-%20Council%20Tax%20Empty%20Homes%20and%20Second%20Homes%20Premiums%20from%202024-2025.pdf),
  [gov.uk guidance](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/long-term-empty-homes-and-second-homes-council-tax-premiums-and-exceptions/guidance-on-the-implementation-of-the-council-tax-premiums-on-long-term-empty-homes-and-second-homes).
  *Honest caveat:* premiums are billed by the district but the cash is split across precepting
  authorities — the calculator uses Exeter's retained share, the narrative shows the gross.
- **[Verified] DIY/construction-waste charges.** Devon charges for soil/rubble (£4.10/bag),
  plasterboard (£7.60/sheet), UPVC, tyres, asbestos etc., with a free allowance protecting
  ordinary household use; van permits limit trade abuse. The county valued the income at
  **~£1m/yr** (lost when the government restricted DIY charging from Dec 2024). Cllr Roger
  Croad's framing is the equity case: *"It doesn't seem right that all council tax payers
  should have to pay for waste from people who can afford to carry out their own home
  improvements."*
  [Devon chargeable waste](https://www.devon.gov.uk/wasteandrecycling/recycling-centres/chargeable-waste/),
  [LetsRecycle](https://www.letsrecycle.com/news/devon-hwrcs-harder-to-maintain-after-diy-charge-removal/).
- **[Verified] Garden-waste subscription** £49 (140L) / £61 (240L) — an opt-in charge on
  garden-owning, broadly more asset-rich households; flats and renters are unaffected.
  [Exeter bin charges](https://exeter.gov.uk/bins-and-recycling/ordering-waste-or-recycling-bins/bin-charges/).
- **[Verified] Leisure — the golf course, not the pool.** Exeter proposed ~£0.6m of leisure
  cuts in 2025 including closing/selling Northbrook **golf course** (golfers skew wealthier —
  the progressive part) — but the same package threatened the **swimming pool and 16 play
  areas**, which are not progressive (see *protect* below).
  [Exeter Observer](https://exeterobserver.org/2025/03/29/city-council-holds-sham-northbrook-swimming-pool-closure-consultation-600000-exeter-leisure-services-budget-cut-st-sidwells-point-drains-other-leisure-sites/).
- **[Verified] Emissions-banded parking permits.** Devon already prices extra resident permits
  by vehicle emissions band (£35 first → £55–£65 for larger/dirtier/additional cars) — a
  replicable charge-by-ability-to-pay proxy.
  [Devon residents' parking](https://www.devon.gov.uk/roads-and-transport/parking/parking-permits/residents-parking/).
- **[Verified] Means-tested adult social-care charging** is inherently progressive: capital
  above £23,250 pays full cost, a tariff applies between £14,250–£23,250, and a Minimum Income
  Guarantee protects the poorest; deferred-payment agreements draw care costs against housing
  wealth (£400 fee + interest).
  [Devon community charging policy](https://www.devon.gov.uk/adult-social-care/document/charging-policy-for-community-based-services-for-adults/).

## Protect these — the regressive cuts to avoid
The same budget lines contain cuts that fall hardest on those in need. If money must be found,
it comes from the levers above, **not** these:
- **[Reported] Council Tax Reduction / Support.** Localised cuts since 2013 already pushed
  bills onto **1.3m** low-income working-age households (a ~**£630m / 14%** real-terms cut),
  with four-fifths of councils now demanding minimum payments — widely called a "new poll tax."
  [IFS R153](https://ifs.org.uk/publications/impacts-localised-council-tax-support-schemes),
  [CPAG](https://cpag.org.uk/news-blogs/news-listings/new-poll-tax-londons-poorest-and-councils-hit-hard-council-tax-changes).
- **[Verified] Swimming pools & children's play areas** (Northbrook pool + 16 play areas) —
  used most by low-income families.
  [Exeter Observer](https://exeterobserver.org/2025/03/29/city-council-holds-sham-northbrook-swimming-pool-closure-consultation-600000-exeter-leisure-services-budget-cut-st-sidwells-point-drains-other-leisure-sites/).
- **[Reported] Subsidised rural buses & concessionary travel** — cutting socially-necessary
  routes hits the isolated, elderly, disabled and car-less rural poor hardest; the
  concessionary pass is itself broadly progressive.
  [Exeter Observer — Devon 2025/26 budget](https://exeterobserver.org/2025/01/14/devon-county-council-2025-26-budget-22-million-service-delivery-cuts-concealed-60-million-costs-increases/).
- **[Verified] Statutory & post-16 home-to-school transport** — charge for discretionary
  spare seats (~£800/yr), but keep the core entitlement that rural low-income families depend on.
  [Devon school & college transport](https://www.devon.gov.uk/children-families-education/education/school-and-college-transport/post-16-transport/).

## Honest framing
These levers are real and largely already adopted, but individually modest (Exeter's retained
premium share is ~£99k/yr; Devon's DIY recovery ~£1m and partly constrained). They are a
**fairer first round of cuts**, not a budget fix — the durable money comes from the owned
assets in `revenue-levers.md`. The discipline is simple: ask more of wealth and assets, protect
the means-tested floor, and be honest that some painful cuts (services used by the better-off)
are the right ones to make first.
